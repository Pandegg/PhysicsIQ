The Setup

A spring is fixed to a wall. A block is attached to the other end. The surface is perfectly frictionless. Right now, nothing is moving. The spring is at its natural length.

Spring at natural length. Block at rest. Net force = zero.
This position — where the net force on the block is exactly zero — is the equilibrium position. Place the block here and leave it alone, it stays. The moment you disturb it, everything changes.

The History
England, 1676 — Robert Hooke
Robert Hooke spent years measuring springs with great care. He stretched them by different amounts and recorded the forces required. He found something clean and beautiful: the relationship was perfectly proportional. Stretch a spring twice as far — it pulls back twice as hard. Three times as far — three times as hard. He published this finding as an anagram to protect his priority, then revealed it plainly three years later. We now call it Hooke's Law.

The harder you pull, the harder the spring resists. And the resistance is perfectly proportional to how far you have pulled.

Double the displacement — double the restoring force.
Triple the displacement — triple the restoring force.
The spring's response is always exactly proportional to how far you have disturbed it.

Why the minus sign matters
The minus sign means force always points opposite to displacement. Pull right — force points left. Push left — force points right. Without this sign, \(F = kx\) would mean the spring pushes the block further away the more it is displaced. That produces runaway motion, not oscillation. The minus sign is the physics.
\[ F = -kx \]
F — restoring force, in newtons. Always toward equilibrium.
x — displacement from equilibrium, in metres.
k — spring constant, in N/m. The stiffness.
– — force always opposes displacement. Always.

What happens at the extremes of k?

If k = 0
The spring offers no resistance. No restoring force. The block, once displaced, never returns. No oscillation — just drift.
If k approaches infinity (a rigid rod)
The spring cannot stretch. No displacement means no restoring force. Again, no oscillation.
If k is finite and nonzero
The spring deforms — but resists deformation. This tension between yielding and resisting is exactly what produces oscillation.
k is not just a number. It is the physical personality of the spring — its willingness to deform and its determination to return.
Discovering SHM

We know \(F = -kx\) from Hooke's Law. We also know Newton's Second Law: \(F = ma\). Both describe the same force on the block. Combining:

\[ ma = -kx \]

Dividing both sides by \(m\):

\[ a = -\frac{k}{m}\, x \]

Now stop. Look at this carefully. Acceleration is proportional to displacement. The direction is always opposite. And what is the defining condition for Simple Harmonic Motion?

Exactly this. Acceleration proportional to displacement, opposite in direction. Therefore this motion IS Simple Harmonic Motion.

But notice — we did not begin by assuming SHM. We began with a spring. We applied Hooke's Law. We applied Newton's Second Law. SHM emerged as a consequence.

We discovered it. We did not assume it.
The Hidden Constant

Look at \(a = -\dfrac{k}{m}\,x\) again. The quantity \(\dfrac{k}{m}\) is sitting quietly, doing enormous work.

The spring constant \(k\) does not change. The mass \(m\) does not change. Therefore \(\dfrac{k}{m}\) is a fixed number for this system — it does not change as the block moves, does not change with displacement, does not change with time. It determines the urgency of the return.

What if the force were not linear — say \(F = -kx^2\)? Then \(a = -\dfrac{k}{m}x^2\), and the ratio \(\dfrac{a}{x} = -\dfrac{k}{m}x\) would change with every position. The SHM condition — that \(\dfrac{a}{x}\) must be constant — would fail. Linearity of \(F = -kx\) is what keeps \(\dfrac{k}{m}\) constant throughout the motion.

In SHM, we use a quantity called angular frequency — symbol \(\omega\), measured in radians per second — to describe how rapidly the oscillation repeats. Comparing \(a = -\dfrac{k}{m}x\) with the standard SHM form \(a = -\omega^2 x\):

\[ \omega = \sqrt{\dfrac{k}{m}} \]
\(\omega\) = angular frequency in rad/s. Large \(\omega\) = fast oscillation. Small \(\omega\) = slow.
Your Prediction

We know \(\omega = \sqrt{k/m}\). We also know the relationship between angular frequency and time period:

\[ \omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} \]
Using these two equations, what is the time period \(T\)?
The Formula — Earned

From \(\omega = 2\pi/T\) and \(\omega = \sqrt{k/m}\): substituting gives \(2\pi/T = \sqrt{k/m}\), therefore:

\[ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{m}{k}} \]
\(2\pi\) — one full oscillation = one full circle = \(2\pi\) radians. Geometry, not convention.
\(m\) in numerator — more mass, more inertia, slower acceleration, longer period.
\(k\) in denominator — stiffer spring, larger force, faster acceleration, shorter period.
Amplitude absent — not hidden, not small. Completely absent. This is about to surprise you.
The Big Surprise

Two identical spring-block systems. Same spring. Same block. Only one difference:

System A
Pulled 2 cm and released
System B
Pulled 10 cm and released
Which system completes one full oscillation faster?
Isochronism

Both take exactly the same time. Every single time.

This feels wrong. System B travels five times the distance. Surely it must need more time? Here is what your intuition is missing: System B also experiences five times the restoring force at every point along its path. More distance — but faster motion.

These two effects cancel with perfect precision. Why? Because \(F = -kx\) is linear. Doubling the amplitude exactly doubles both the distance and the peak speed. The ratio — which determines time — stays constant.

If force were proportional to \(x^2\), this cancellation would not be exact. Period would depend on amplitude. Motion would still be oscillatory but not simple harmonic.
Galileo Galilei — Late 16th Century
Galileo observed a chandelier swinging in a cathedral. He noticed it took the same time for each swing — whether the swing was large or small. He timed it using his own pulse. He called this isochronism — equal time, regardless of amplitude.
\[ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{m}{k}} \]
Amplitude does not appear. Not hidden. Not small. Absent.
The Traps

These are the mistakes that appear in almost every examination. Meet them now, before the exam does.

"If I pull the block further, the period must increase."

This feels right — more distance should mean more time.

More distance also means more restoring force, which means faster motion. These cancel exactly. \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}\) — amplitude is absent.
"A heavier block oscillates faster — gravity pulls it back harder."

This feels right — heavier means more gravitational pull.

\(F = -kx\) has no mass in it. The spring does not know how heavy the block is. A heavier block has the same restoring force but more inertia. It accelerates more slowly. Heavier always means slower.
"The minus sign in \(F = -kx\) is just a mathematical convention."

Minus signs often just indicate direction.

Remove the minus sign and \(F = kx\) means the spring pushes the block further away the more it is displaced. Runaway motion, not oscillation. The minus sign is the physics — not notation.
ISC Pattern 1 — Direct Calculation
Question: A block of 0.5 kg is attached to a spring of constant 200 N/m on a frictionless surface. Find the time period of oscillation.
Step 1 — Identify
Given: \(m = 0.5\) kg, \(k = 200\) N/m. Apply \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}\).
Step 2 — Substitute
\(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{0.5}{200}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.0025} = 2\pi \times 0.05\)
Answer
\(T = 2\pi \times 0.05 \approx \mathbf{0.314 \text{ seconds}}\). Always check units — kg, N/m, seconds.
ISC Pattern 2 — Mass Changes
Question: A spring-block system has time period \(T\). The mass is doubled. Find the new time period.
Step 1 — Think proportionally
\(T \propto \sqrt{m}\). Do not substitute numbers. Reason about ratios.
Step 2 — Apply
If \(m\) doubles, \(\sqrt{m}\) increases by \(\sqrt{2}\). Therefore \(T_{new} = \sqrt{2}\,T\).
Answer
\(T_{new} = \sqrt{2}\,T \approx \mathbf{1.41\,T}\). Period increases — heavier block means slower oscillation.
ISC Pattern 3 — The Cut Spring
Question: A spring of constant \(k\) is cut into two equal halves. One half is used with the same block. Find the new time period.
Step 1 — The key insight
A shorter spring has fewer coils. Fewer coils means less total deformation for the same force — so it is stiffer. Half the length means double the stiffness. \(k_{half} = 2k\).
Step 2 — Apply
\(T_{new} = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{m}{2k}} = \dfrac{T}{\sqrt{2}}\)
Answer
\(T_{new} = \dfrac{T}{\sqrt{2}} \approx \mathbf{0.707\,T}\). Period decreases — half-spring is stiffer, oscillates faster.

Energy in SHM

As the block oscillates, energy moves between two forms. When the block moves fast — kinetic energy is high. When the block is momentarily still — potential energy is high. The total stays constant on a frictionless surface.

The Two Forms
\[ KE = \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \]
Kinetic energy — depends on velocity
\[ PE = \dfrac{1}{2}kx^2 \]
Potential energy — depends on displacement from equilibrium
\[ E = \dfrac{1}{2}kA^2 \]
Total energy — depends only on stiffness and amplitude. Constant throughout.
Total energy depends only on \(k\) and \(A\). Not on mass directly. Not on current position. Once you set the amplitude, the total energy is fixed for that oscillation.

Velocity at Any Position

From energy conservation: \(KE = E - PE\)

\[ \dfrac{1}{2}mv^2 = \dfrac{1}{2}k(A^2 - x^2) \]

Solving for \(v\):

\[ v = \omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2} \]
At \(x = 0\): \(v = \omega A\) (maximum)
At \(x = A\): \(v = 0\) (momentarily at rest)
At \(x = A/2\): \(v = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\,\omega A \approx 0.866\,v_{max}\)
At \(x = A/\sqrt{2}\): \(v = \omega A/\sqrt{2} = v_{max}/\sqrt{2}\)

ISC Energy Patterns
Pattern 1: Find KE when displacement \(= A/2\).
Solution
\(KE = \frac{1}{2}k(A^2 - A^2/4) = \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \mathbf{\frac{3E}{4}}\). Three-quarters of energy is kinetic at half-amplitude.
Pattern 2: Find position where \(KE = 2 \times PE\).
Solution
\(\frac{1}{2}k(A^2-x^2) = 2 \times \frac{1}{2}kx^2\). Therefore \(A^2 = 3x^2\). So \(x = \mathbf{A/\sqrt{3}}\).
Pattern 3: Find total energy given \(k = 500\) N/m and \(A = 4\) cm.
Solution
\(E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 500 \times (0.04)^2 = \mathbf{0.4 \text{ J}}\). No velocity needed. No position needed.
Energy Reconstruction

Derive the velocity formula \(v = \omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2}\) from energy conservation alone.

Step 1
Total energy \(E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2\). At position \(x\): \(PE = \frac{1}{2}kx^2\). Therefore \(KE = E - PE = \frac{1}{2}k(A^2 - x^2)\).
Step 2
\(\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}k(A^2 - x^2)\). Therefore \(v^2 = \frac{k}{m}(A^2 - x^2) = \omega^2(A^2 - x^2)\).
Result
\(v = \omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2}\). Complete derivation from energy conservation alone — no calculus required.
Energy in SHM — Complete
Discovery 1 — Two Forms, One Total
\(KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\). \(PE = \frac{1}{2}kx^2\). Total \(E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2\). Total depends only on \(k\) and \(A\) — constant throughout.
Discovery 2 — The Transfer
At mean (\(x=0\)): all kinetic. At extreme (\(x=A\)): all potential. Between: continuous transfer. Nothing lost on a frictionless surface.
Discovery 3 — Equal Energies at \(x = A/\sqrt{2}\)
\(KE = PE\) not at \(x = A/2\) but at \(x = A/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.707A\). Because energy depends on \(x^2\), not \(x\).
Discovery 4 — Velocity Formula
\(v = \omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2}\). Maximum at mean. Zero at extreme. Derived from energy conservation.
